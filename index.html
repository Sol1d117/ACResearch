Overview

Aroostook County, Maine’s northernmost region, is a rural area rooted in agriculture and community heritage. Its population has declined from 106,064 in 1960 to 67,105 in 2020, largely due to outmigration. This page examines how federal education policies, including the Department of Education and student loans, alongside organizations like Maine College Circle, might contribute to this trend by encouraging higher education that leads young people away from the county.

Population Trends

Aroostook County’s population peaked in 1960 and has steadily declined since, with a slight uptick recently:

Demographics: 19.2% under 18, 55.8% aged 18-64, 25.0% 65+ (vs. Maine’s 18.35%, 60.18%, 21.47%). Higher senior and child populations suggest an aging community with fewer working-age adults.

Federal Education Policies

The U.S. Department of Education, formed in 1979, consolidated federal education efforts. The Higher Education Act of 1965 introduced federal student loans and Pell Grants, boosting college access:

  • Impact: Lower-income students from Aroostook (median income $54,254 vs. Maine’s $68,251) can attend college, often out-of-county or out-of-state.
  • Criticism: Standardized policies (e.g., No Child Left Behind) prioritize urban metrics, sidelining rural needs like vocational training, potentially eroding local pride.

Maine College Circle

Founded in 1992, Maine College Circle inspires rural Maine youth to pursue higher education:

  • Programs: Workshops for grades 3-8, 4,337 scholarships awarded, and FAFSA guidance.
  • Reach: Over 60,000 students, including Aroostook communities like Mars Hill.
  • Goal: Early college aspirations, but may lead students to leave for better opportunities elsewhere.

Hypothetical Correlation to Population Decline

Federal policies and Maine College Circle increase college attendance, but Aroostook’s limited job market (e.g., agriculture, healthcare) can’t retain graduates:

  • Outmigration: Young people leave for college and stay in urban areas, contributing to a 36.7% drop since 1960.
  • Local Pride: Federal “one-size-fits-all” tactics disconnect education from Aroostook’s rural identity, weakening community ties.
  • Potential Fix: Tailored policies—vocational focus, local job incentives—could stem the tide.

While education isn’t the sole cause (industry shifts and aging also factor), it amplifies outmigration in a county struggling to keep its youth.
